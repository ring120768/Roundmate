# Cross-Pollination Research — Round-Based Local Service Trades

*Researched 2026-07-10 for Pugsie PA. Global scan (UK primary; US, AUS, NZ for patterns). Purpose: inform the bolt-on pattern and multi-trade expansion decisions.*

---

## Executive summary

Every trade researched — window cleaning, gardening/landscaping, domestic cleaning, ironing/laundry, oven cleaning, mobile valeting — runs the same underlying business: a **round of recurring regulars** (the cashflow base) plus **higher-ticket one-off add-ons** (the margin). The add-ons are where trades cross-pollinate: a window cleaner's gutter clear is a gardener's gutter clear is a pressure washer's gutter clear. The kit, the customer list, and the trust relationship transfer; only the service label changes.

Three findings matter most for Pugsie PA:

**First, add-ons are counter-seasonal by design.** Gutters peak in autumn/winter, pressure washing in spring/summer, ovens before Christmas, BBQs before summer, Christmas lights in Nov–Dec. Tradespeople use add-ons to smooth the year — which means a seasonal reminder/campaign engine is not a Christmas-lights feature, it's THE generic bolt-on feature.

**Second, everything reduces to two admin patterns.** Pattern one: recurring round job, fixed price, regenerates on completion (windows 4-weekly, valeting fortnightly, ovens annually — same pattern, different cadence). Pattern two: one-off variable-price job (gutter clear, garden clearance, full detail) that may need a lightweight quote. Every competitor (Squeegee, Jobber, CleanerPlanner, ServiceM8) is some blend of these two pipelines. Pugsie already has pattern one; pattern two needs only a service type + one-off flag + price, not a heavyweight quote engine.

**Third, the expansion ladder is real and documented.** The US proves it at scale: Christmas Decor was founded by a landscaper to avoid winter layoffs and is now a ~$87M network; 46% of US landscapers do snow removal as a sideline; Jobber's data across 350k+ service pros shows optional add-on line items get 25–50% take-up. The UK equivalents (solar panel cleaning at ~10.5% CAGR, Airbnb changeovers on 101M guest nights/year) are earlier but growing.

## The cross-pollination map

Each trade's documented add-on menu, with typical UK prices. The baseline job is what the round is built on; add-ons are multiples of it.

### Window cleaners (baseline: £20–£40 per 3-bed clean, 4–8 weekly)

| Add-on | Typical UK price | Cadence | Value vs baseline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gutter clearing | £65–£125 | 1–2x/year | 3–4x |
| Gutter/fascia/soffit wash | £80–£250 | annual | 4–8x |
| Conservatory roof clean | £80–£250+ (£10–£20/panel) | 1–2x/year | 4–10x |
| Driveway pressure wash | £150–£400 | one-off/annual, Mar–Aug | 6–15x |
| Patio jet wash | £75–£200 | one-off/annual | 3–7x |
| Solar panel clean | £80–£200 (median ~£140) | annual (UK) | 4–6x |
| Christmas lights install | ~£350 avg UK (£100–£1,000); US $1,200–$1,500 | annual seasonal | 10x+ |

Gutters are the universal first add-on: lowest barrier (~£1.5k gutter-vac kit), naturally recurring, and forum consensus calls conservatory roofs "the best-paying add-on alongside gutter clearing." Solar is the fastest-growing UK niche — window cleaners already own the water-fed-pole skillset and the customer list.

### Gardeners / landscapers (baseline: £20–£40/hr, weekly–monthly rounds Mar–Sep)

| Add-on | Typical UK price | Season |
|---|---|---|
| Hedge trimming | avg ~£259/job (£90/hr) | Mar–Sep + dormant-season cuts |
| Leaf clearance | £60–£150/visit | Oct–Nov |
| Gutter clearing | £50–£165+ | autumn/winter |
| Garden clearance | £200–£250/day | year-round, autumn peak |
| Pressure washing | £100–£500 | year-round, winter-viable |
| Fence painting | ~£26/panel | spring/summer |
| Turfing | £10–£30/m² | spring & autumn |
| Christmas lights | ~£350 avg | Nov–Jan |

Winter is the existential problem: UK gardener income drops roughly 37–50% Nov–Feb (sources vary; direction is solid). Documented fillers: hard landscaping, gutter clearing, pressure washing, clearances, Christmas lights, and switching rounds from weekly to monthly. Jim's Mowing (NZ/AUS) makes gutter clearing and pressure washing standard franchise services — the gardener/window-cleaner add-on menus literally overlap.

### Domestic cleaners / housekeepers (baseline: £15–£22/hr, weekly/fortnightly)

Add-ons split into two tiers with different admin shapes. Same-visit extras (oven interior, fridge, inside windows, ironing, bed changes) are flat-fee line items on the regular job. Standalone premium jobs are fixed-price one-offs:

| Premium job | Typical UK price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| End-of-tenancy clean | £180–£420 | fixed by property size; oven included |
| Deep/spring clean | £180–£600+ | ~30–50% hourly premium |
| Oven clean | £50–£105 | often subbed to specialists |
| Carpet clean | £30–£60/room | kit-investment barrier |
| Airbnb changeover | £50–£120/turnover | recurring, deadline-driven, fixed fee |

Airbnb changeovers are the standout growth niche: UK short-term-let guest nights hit 101M in 2025 (+12% YoY, ONS-derived). Every turnover is a clean on a hard deadline at a fixed fee — recurring, schedule-dense, and geographically concentrated (Cornwall, Lakes, Edinburgh, London). Molly Maid and Poppies now market it as a named service line.

### Ironing / laundry services

Structurally a round business: collection/delivery runs, minimum orders (£20–£33), priced per item (~£2.80 avg) or per kg (£5–£9). Bundling runs both directions — ironing businesses add laundry/dry-cleaning; cleaning platforms sell ironing as an in-visit extra. Pugsie's round-grouping maps onto collection rounds directly.

### Oven cleaners (incl. BBQ) — baseline: £50–£105 per oven, fixed menu

The most Pugsie-shaped trade of all. Ovenclean's own franchise material describes building a **"milk-round of regular, repeat customers"** who book the next clean on the day of service — the complete-and-rebook flow is literally the franchise playbook. Fixed price per appliance (single ~£75, double ~£90, range £60–£125, Aga £140–£160), add-ons per item (hob £15–£25, extractor £15–£20, microwave £15–£35, BBQ £55–£150), rebook cycle 6–12 months. Seasonality is sharp: fully booked weeks ahead pre-Christmas, dead Jan–Mar, with BBQ cleaning (spring/autumn) as the counter-seasonal filler. Because the rebook gap is long, reminders matter more here than in any other trade.

### Mobile valeting / car wash — baseline: £15–£30 wash, £35–£80 mini valet

Two customer types: regulars on weekly–monthly maintenance washes (several operators now run subscriptions at £15–£50/month) and one-off full valets (£90–£145) or details (£150–£550+, the only quote-priced tier). Corporate car-park rounds are a real segment. Winter forum consensus: valeters **with a solid regular round are fine; those relying on ad-hoc calls struggle** — the strongest third-party validation of Pugsie's core premise found in this research. Kit overlaps heavily with window cleaning (van, water tank, pressure washer, pure-water systems).

### Adjacent trades: fencing and general handyman (partial fits)

**Garden fencing** fits as a bolt-on, not a trade. In the research it shows up as landscaper project work (fence repair/replacement) and as a paint/stain add-on (~£26/panel painted, ~£30 stained) — quoted, one-off, weather-dependent, spring/summer. It's a classic winter-gap or upsell job for a gardener with round customers, and repainting every 2–3 years gives it a long rebook cycle like ovens. A gardener using Pugsie would handle it as a one-off priced job on an existing customer; no new features needed beyond the one-off pattern.

**General handyman** is the boundary case. It's round-adjacent, not round-based: work is reactive ("my gate's broken") rather than scheduled, almost everything is quoted, and there's no natural repeat cadence. That said, the multi-service giants are built on it — Fantastic Services includes handyman among its 100+ services, Neighborly runs Mr. Handyman as one of its 28 cross-referring brands, and UK handyman outfits (e.g. Willow Alexander, London) sell Christmas light installation. The honest read: a handyman could run on Pugsie (customers, jobs, invoices, payments all apply), but the app's differentiators — rounds, auto-rebook, Smart Rounds — mean less to him, and he's better served by quote-led tools like ServiceM8 or Tradify. Handyman customers are, however, a cross-sell *audience*: the same homeowner who books a fence repair books gutter clears and pressure washes. Verdict: support handymen incidentally, don't target them; fencing folds into the gardener/landscaper menu.

**Locksmiths** (added 2026-07-10, analysis rather than researched) are a weaker fit still: the core trade is emergency callouts — reactive, quoted, no cadence, no rounds. The exception is B2B: landlords and letting agents generate recurring lock-change work at every tenancy changeover, portfolio-wide. A locksmith fits Pugsie only through the work-provider pattern below.

### The work-provider pattern: estate/letting agents as B2B accounts

The end-of-tenancy market (£180–£420/job) is commissioned as much by letting agents as by tenants, and the same channel feeds locksmiths (changeover lock changes), gardeners (between-tenancy tidy-ups), and gutter/maintenance work. Airbnb management companies are the same shape. Structurally this is a different kind of customer: one account (the agent) generating repeat jobs at **many different property addresses**, on an agreed price list by property size, paid via monthly consolidated invoice on account terms rather than per job at the door. Pugsie's current model assumes customer = one person at one address; supporting B2B work providers means an account entity that owns multiple job addresses and batch invoicing. Not MVP — but a known schema decision to make deliberately when a cleaner or locksmith user first asks for it. For a solo cleaner, one letting agent relationship can be the equivalent of a whole round.

**Booking idea (2026-07-10):** the seamless-booking play here is NOT Calendly-style slot-picking (agents would claim time the tradesperson needs to control). It's a per-agent job-request link: agent submits property, size and date needed → lands as a pending job auto-priced from that agent's price list → tradesperson confirms and slots it → agent gets automatic confirmation, jobs roll up to a monthly consolidated invoice. No UK round-software competitor offers this; "give your letting agent this link" could be the feature that wins B2B accounts. Later phase, alongside the work-provider schema.

**Design principle (agreed 2026-07-10): request → confirm, on the tradesman's rails.** The same request-link pattern extends to consumer customers, but it is never slot-picking. Customers submit a request; the tradesman confirms the visit. The tradesman sets the parameters the request runs on: minimum lead time, working days, request granularity (date range / week commencing, not a time slot), daily capacity, and — the Pugsie-only twist — Smart Rounds steering, where the form offers dates the tradesman is already due in that postcode area. Customer feels in control; the round stays tight. This fits the psychology of the target user: diary control is non-negotiable for round-based trades.

## The seasonal calendar

| Season | Busy | Gap-fillers documented |
|---|---|---|
| Spring (Mar–May) | gardening rounds restart, pressure washing peak begins, turfing | BBQ cleans, spring deep cleans, conservatory roofs |
| Summer (Jun–Aug) | all rounds at peak; pressure washing +10–25% pricing | holiday-let changeovers peak |
| Autumn (Sep–Nov) | leaf clearance, gutter season starts, Christmas lights installs begin (Sep–Nov) | oven cleans ramp toward Christmas |
| Winter (Dec–Feb) | oven pre-Christmas rush, Christmas lights, gutters | hard landscaping, clearances, interior valets, winter-protection packages; gardener income drops ~40% without fillers |

The pattern: no single trade is busy year-round, but the **combined** menu is. That's the economic engine behind cross-pollination — and behind a multi-trade admin app.

## Documented expansion cases

**Christmas Decor (US)** — the canonical case. Founded 1986 by landscaper Blake Smith explicitly to avoid winter layoffs; lighting became more profitable than the landscaping and he exited the original trade. Now ~325 markets, network revenue ~$87M (2023), average residential install ~$1,800, franchise average ~$205k gross in a ~75-day season, ~15% margins. Majority of franchisees were already seasonal trade businesses when they joined.

**Shine / Shack Shine (US/Canada)** — window cleaning franchises whose whole pitch is windows-in-summer + lights-in-winter. One Shack Shine franchisee reportedly grew lighting revenue $150k → $927k (single-source vendor anecdote — treat with caution).

**Fantastic Services (UK/AUS)** — started ~2009 as a London cleaning company; now 100+ property services, 530+ franchisees, ~£30M revenue, built on £20M+ of its own booking/CRM tech. The strongest evidence that one brand + one admin system can span many trades — and that the software layer is what makes it possible.

**Snow removal (US/Canada)** — 46% of US landscape contractors do snow & ice management; over half of all snow/ice providers are lawn/landscape firms moonlighting. Not UK-relevant directly, but proves "core round + winter bolt-on" is standard industry behaviour, not an outlier.

**UK solar cleaning** — UK installed solar passed 18 GW in 2025 heading for 50+ GW by 2035; panel-cleaning market projected ~10.5% CAGR. Trade press names window cleaners as the natural entrants. Named case studies are thin — the evidence is in operator service lists, not interviews.

## What this means for Pugsie PA

**The data model needs exactly three things it doesn't fully have yet.** A `service_type` on jobs (window clean, gutter clear, oven, valet…) with an optional default price per customer; a clean one-off vs recurring distinction (already mostly there via visit frequency); and support for long cadences (6/12-monthly) alongside short ones (weekly/4-weekly). CleanerPlanner's "add job → pick service → tick one-off → type price" is the simplest credible model on the market and covers 80% of the value. No quote engine needed for MVP — a lightweight "price TBC → priced → booked" status on a job mimics what solo operators actually do. Full quoting (Jobber-style optional line items, good/better/best) is a later phase; Jobber's data says optional add-ons convert at 25–50%, so it's worth doing eventually.

**Christmas lights should be built as the first instance of a generic "seasonal campaign" pattern.** The mechanic is identical across trades: segment customers by service history ("everyone I did X for last year"), send a templated reminder at the right month, track booked/declined. Christmas lights (Oct–Nov), gutters (Sep), BBQs (Apr), ovens (Oct–Nov pre-Christmas), winter valet protection (Oct) — one feature, five trades. This is a single query over the existing `jobs` + `messages` tables plus a template and a status. Phase 9 should be renamed in spirit from "Christmas Lights CRM" to "Seasonal Campaigns (Christmas lights first)."

**The competitor gap Pugsie fits is real.** UK round software is either cheap-but-dated (Aworka £10–12/mo, no campaigns), capable-but-dense (Squeegee from ~£16/mo, campaigns gated to higher tiers, widely called overwhelming for solo operators), stagnant (CleanerPlanner £30/mo), or US-priced and not round-native (Jobber $39–$199/mo). A simple, mobile-first, UK-priced app with the core loop + seasonal campaigns is the open slot. Positioning benchmarks: Jobber claims 7+ hrs/week saved; Squeegee reviewers cite ~5 hrs/week.

**Trade expansion order, if/when the time comes.** Oven cleaning and mobile valeting are the closest structural matches to the existing app (fixed-price menus, rounds, rebook-on-completion — ovens are almost zero new features, just longer frequencies and reminders). Gardeners need the one-off/quoted-job pattern and winter campaign tools. Domestic cleaners need per-visit extras (line items) and the Airbnb changeover niche. Ironing rounds work with what exists today. All of this stays parked behind the rule already in the ROADMAP: window cleaners first, until they're using it daily and paying.

## Confidence notes

Well-supported: the add-on menus themselves (consistent across franchise sites, cost guides, and operator price lists in four countries); UK price ranges (Checkatrade/MyJobQuote corroborate); Christmas Decor's history and scale (trade press + franchise analytics); Fantastic Services' model (first-party + trade press); US snow-removal percentages (trade surveys); UK winter income drop for gardeners (direction, not exact %); Ovenclean's milk-round rebook model (first-party franchise material); ONS-derived short-term-let growth.

Vendor-sourced / treat as directional: Jobber's "35% revenue uplift from bundling" and "25–50% upsell take rates" (self-published, data-based but marketing-flavoured); holiday-lighting profit figures ($16k in two months etc.); the $927k Shack Shine anecdote (single source).

Not found anywhere: a published "% of window cleaners offering add-ons" survey; any study quantifying multi-service customer churn vs single-service ("2+ services churn less" is inference from Neighborly/Jobber behaviour, not citation); UK Christmas-lights market size (existence proof only).
